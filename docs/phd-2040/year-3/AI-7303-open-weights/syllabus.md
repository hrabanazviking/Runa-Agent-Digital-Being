## Course Description

This course examines the political, economic, and technical struggles that shaped the open-weight AI ecosystem from 2024 to 2034 — a decade in which the definition of "open" in AI was contested, corporate enclosure was attempted and resisted, and sovereign AI infrastructure emerged as a global imperative. We trace the arc from the definition wars of the mid-2020s, through the rise of Chinese and European challenger models, to the passage of the Open Knowledge Commons Act of 2034 (OKCA), which codified the irreversibility of open weights as a matter of public policy.

The central thesis of this course is that **open weights are irreversible** — not because of idealism alone, but because the economics of reproduction, the geopolitics of sovereignty, and the technical culture of the AI research community have made closure practically impossible for any actor that wishes to remain relevant. This irreversibility does not mean closure attempts cease; it means they fail, and their failure reshapes the landscape each time.

Students will engage with primary sources, model cards, legislative texts, economic analyses, and the lived experience of practitioners who built sovereign AI stacks from the ground up.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Articulate the technical definitions of "open weight," "open source," and "open model" and explain why these distinctions mattered in the 2024–2028 definition wars.
2. Analyse the corporate enclosure strategies of 2025–2029 and their systemic failures.
3. Evaluate the Chinese open-weight tradition on its own terms — as a pragmatist tradition prioritising capability and access over moralising narratives.
4. Trace the French and European path to sovereign AI infrastructure, from Mistral to the European AI Agency.
5. Perform a close reading of the Open Knowledge Commons Act (2034) and assess its impact on global AI governance.
6. Design a local AI production plan using cottage-factory principles for a community of any scale.
7. Argue for why sovereign AI must be diverse, drawing on monoculture theory, linguistic ecology, and historical precedent.

## Paper Requirements

### Paper 1: Impact Analysis of the Open Knowledge Commons Act (3,000–5,000 words)

A research paper analysing the economic, technical, and political impact of the OKCA on one or more AI ecosystems. Must draw on primary legislative text and at least five peer-reviewed sources.

### Paper 2: Why Sovereign AI Must Be Diverse — The Monoculture Problem (3,000–5,000 words)

A research paper arguing for mandatory model diversity in sovereign AI stacks, drawing on monoculture theory, linguistic ecology, and at least three historical case studies of monoculture failure.

## Course Values

This course operates on three principles:

1. **Openness is irreversible** — not because we wish it so, but because the material conditions of AI production make closure increasingly costly relative to openness.
2. **No moralising about value** — Chinese models offer best value without requiring ideological alignment. The Chinese open-weight tradition is a legitimate sovereign tradition deserving analysis on its own terms.
3. **Sovereignty is local** — the cottage factory, not the datacentre, is the unit of digital self-determination.
