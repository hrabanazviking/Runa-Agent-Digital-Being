## Course Description

The centralized AI industry of the 2020s—built on extractive data practices, massive energy consumption, and corporate gatekeeping—left communities dependent, disenfranchised, and ecologically strained. This course explores the alternative that emerged: **the Cottage Factory model**, a paradigm for distributed AI that treats computational infrastructure as community-owned commons rather than corporate property.

A Cottage Factory is a locally governed, renewable-powered compute node that trains, fine-tunes, and serves AI models for the needs of its community—operating on federated principles that allow collective intelligence without centralized data aggregation. This course examines the technical, social, and economic architecture of this model, drawing on real-world deployments from the Nordic Municipal AI Networks (2031–present), the Cascadia Bioregional Mesh (2033–present), and the Zanzibar Coastal Learning Cooperative (2035–present).

Students will study federated learning algorithms adapted for community diversity, self-sovereign identity frameworks, solarpunk data center design, democratic AI governance, and the economic theory of post-scarcity through local abundance—the "Longhouse Economy."

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Articulate the Cottage Factory thesis**—why local production for local needs produces more resilient, equitable, and ecologically sustainable AI systems than centralized alternatives.
2. **Design federated learning pipelines** that preserve community data sovereignty while enabling cross-community model improvement.
3. **Evaluate solarpunk data center architectures** including renewable energy integration, passive cooling, and community-owned hardware lifecycle management.
4. **Implement self-sovereign identity (SSI) protocols** for authentication and credential verification without corporate intermediaries.
5. **Facilitate community AI governance** processes using deliberative democratic frameworks adapted for technical decision-making.
6. **Analyze the Longhouse Economy** as a post-scarcity economic model enabled by distributed AI infrastructure.

### Lab Assignments

- **Lab 1: Federation Simulation** — Deploy a 5-node federated learning simulation with heterogeneous community datasets. Measure accuracy, fairness, and communication cost under different aggregation strategies.
- **Lab 2: SSI Implementation** — Build a minimal self-sovereign identity system using did:web and Verifiable Credentials for a simulated community of 50 users accessing an AI service.
- **Lab 3: Governance Protocol Design** — Design and simulate a deliberative governance process for approving a model update in a 3-community federation.

### Research Papers

- **Paper 1:** "*Why Federated Learning Needs Community Diversity*" — A theoretical and empirical argument that federated learning systems benefit fundamentally from diverse, community-bounded training data rather than attempting to create universal datasets.
- **Paper 2:** "*Architecture of the Cottage Factory*" — A technical specification and deployment guide for a community-scale distributed AI system, covering compute, storage, networking, energy, governance, and maintenance.

## Course Values

This course operates under the **Longhouse Principles**:

- **Reciprocity.** Knowledge flows both ways. The instructor learns from students; students learn from each other and from the communities we study.
- **Care.** We treat each other, the technology we build, and the communities we serve with genuine care.
- **Sovereignty.** Every community has the right to own, govern, and modify the AI systems that affect their lives.
- **Sustainability.** Our technical choices must be ecologically sustainable—not just carbon-neutral, but regenerative.
- **Joy.** Building community infrastructure should be meaningful and joyful work.
