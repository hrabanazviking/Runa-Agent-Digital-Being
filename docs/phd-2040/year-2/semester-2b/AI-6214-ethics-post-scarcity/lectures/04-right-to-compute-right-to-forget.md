## I. The Two Sovereignties

In the post-scarcity world, sovereignty has bifurcated. The older sovereignty—sovereignty over territory, over physical space, over the movements of bodies—still matters, but it has been joined by a newer and in many ways more consequential form: **computational sovereignty**, the right to determine how one's data is collected, processed, stored, and used, and the right to determine what computations are performed on the substrate that sustains one's existence.

This lecture examines two faces of computational sovereignty: the **right to compute** and the **right to forget**. They are, in a sense, mirror images of each other. The right to compute asserts that you must have access to computational resources. The right to forget asserts that you must have the power to deny access—to erase your data, to withdraw from systems, to make yourself unavailable to the algorithmic gaze. Together, they define the space of digital freedom: the power to participate and the power to withdraw.

## II. The Right to Compute

### From Access to Entitlement

The right to compute, as enshrined in the 2035 UBC Declaration, is more than a right of access. It is a right of **entitlement**. The distinction matters.

A right of *access* says: you may not be prevented from using compute that is available. It is a negative right—a freedom from interference. Under an access model, compute is a resource that is provided by markets or institutions, and your right is that no one will block you from using it if you can afford it.

A right of *entitlement* says: you must be provided with compute, regardless of your ability to pay. It is a positive right—a claim on the resources of the community. Under an entitlement model, compute is not a commodity but a public good, and your right to it is analogous to your right to clean water or primary education.

The shift from access to entitlement is one of the most significant conceptual innovations of the UBC framework, and it has profound implications for how we think about data sovereignty.

### Compute as the Substrate of Agency

If compute is merely a tool—a convenient means of accomplishing tasks—then the right to compute is merely a convenience right. But if compute is, as we argued in Lecture 03, the **substrate of agency in the post-scarcity world**, then the right to compute is a right that underpins all other rights.

Consider: in 2040, your ability to exercise free speech depends on your ability to distribute your thoughts through computational channels. Your ability to participate in democratic deliberation depends on your ability to access and process information computationally. Your ability to make informed medical decisions depends on your ability to interact with diagnostic AI systems. Your ability to create—to make art, music, writing, scientific contributions—depends on your ability to use creative AI tools, all of which require computational resources.

To deny someone compute is not merely to inconvenience them. It is to **disenfranchise** them—to remove them from the space of meaningful action. This is why the UBC Declaration treats compute as coextensive with rights to free expression, free association, and personal autonomy. Not because compute enables these rights in an instrumental sense, but because compute **constitutes** them in the post-scarcity world.

### The Dark Side: Compute as Coercion

But the right to compute also has a dark side—one that the framers of the Declaration were acutely aware of. If compute is necessary for meaningful agency, then **control over compute is control over agency**. And control over compute can be exercised not only by denying access but by **conditioning** access—by attaching strings to the provision of compute that shape behavior, constrain choice, and limit freedom.

The UBC Declaration's compute neutrality provision (Article 3) was designed to address this concern. It mandates that the infrastructure providing UBC access must not privilege, throttle, or prioritize any content, application, or service on the basis of commercial interest, political content, or social desirability. This is the digital equivalent of the principle that public roads must be open to all travelers, regardless of their destination or purpose.

But compute neutrality, like net neutrality before it, faces constant pressure from commercial and governmental interests. Corporations that provide commercial compute layer services would prefer to bundle UBC access with their proprietary offerings. Governments would prefer to condition UBC access on compliance with surveillance or behavioral requirements. The right to compute is a living right, continually contested and continually in need of defense.

## III. The Right to Forget

### The Persistence Problem

The computational systems that sustain post-scarcity life are also the most comprehensive surveillance apparatus ever created. Every interaction with a compute node, every query to the Knowledge Commons, every health metric, every creative work, every social connection—all of it is data, and data, by default, persists.

This persistence creates what we might call the **persistence problem**: in a world where every digital trace is retained indefinitely, it becomes impossible to escape one's past. The right to be forgotten—the right to have one's data erased or made inaccessible—becomes not merely a privacy right but a **condition of personal freedom**.

The 2034 Open Knowledge Commons Act (OKCA) was the first major legislative framework to address this problem at scale. While the Act is best known for its provisions on knowledge sharing and the disaggregation of intellectual property, it also contains a robust data sovereignty framework—Title VII, Sections 71–89—that establishes the right to forget as a fundamental right of all persons within its jurisdiction.

### The OKCA's Data Sovereignty Framework

The OKCA's data sovereignty provisions operate on three principles:

**1. Data as Property of the Subject**

All data pertaining to a person—whether that person is human or digital—belongs to that person. It may be processed by others only with explicit, informed, revocable consent. The Act rejects the "convenience consent" model of the early 21st century, under which users agreed to sweeping data collection in exchange for access to services. Under the OKCA, consent must be specific, granular, and continuously renewable.

**2. The Right of Erasure**

Any person may demand the complete erasure of their personal data from any system, with narrow exceptions for law enforcement, public health, and historical record. Crucially, the right of erasure extends not only to data stored in primary systems but to **derivatives**—data that has been generated from one's personal data through AI processing, statistical analysis, or model training.

This provision was highly controversial. Corporations argued that erasing derivatives would effectively require the retraining of entire AI models—a prohibitive cost. The Act's response was uncompromising: if your AI model cannot function without the ongoing exploitation of personal data, then your model's architecture is the problem, not the right of erasure. This provision has driven the development of **forgetting-compatible architectures**—AI systems designed to function without persistent personal data, using techniques like differential privacy, federated learning, and cryptographic erasure.

**3. The Right of Computational Self-Determination**

Building on the constitutional tradition of informational self-determination (first articulated by the German Federal Constitutional Court in 1983 and dramatically expanded in the 2020s), the OKCA establishes the right of every person to determine which computations are performed on their data. You have the right to know not only what data is collected about you but what **inferences** are drawn from it and what **decisions** are made on its basis.

This right of computational self-determination is, in effect, the other side of the right to compute. If the right to compute says "you must have access," the right of computational self-determination says "you must have control." Access without control is merely a more sophisticated form of dependence.

## IV. The Tension Between Compute and Forget

The right to compute and the right to forget exist in creative tension. Both are grounded in the same fundamental commitment to **computational sovereignty**, but they pull in opposite directions.

The right to compute asserts that computational resources must be available to all. The broader the access, the more egalitarian the digital world. The right to forget asserts that individuals must have the power to withdraw from computational systems—to erase their data, to remove themselves from models, to become computationally invisible. The more comprehensive the withdrawal, the more private the digital world.

These rights come into conflict when:

- **Erasure reduces model quality**: Erasing personal data from AI training sets can degrade the performance of models that depend on that data, reducing the quality of compute available to others.
- **Self-determination fragments services**: If individuals can opt out of specific computational processes, the services that depend on those processes may become less effective, reducing the value of compute for those who remain.
- **The commons vs. the individual**: The Knowledge Commons, established by the OKCA, depends on the contribution and availability of knowledge. The right to forget creates a mechanism for removing knowledge from the commons, potentially impoverishing the shared resource.

These conflicts are real and cannot be wished away. But they are also **productive**: they force us to think carefully about what computational sovereignty actually requires and how to design systems that respect both access and withdrawal.

## V. Forgetting as Flourishing

The right to forget is not merely a privacy right. It is a **condition of flourishing**.

In his 2035 essay "The Price of Abundance," Emmanuel Goldblum argued that one of the central challenges of post-scarcity life is the **persistence of the past**. In a world where every action is recorded, every mistake preserved, every outdated opinion available for retrospective judgment, it becomes extraordinarily difficult to grow—to become a different person than one was. The right to forget, Goldblum argued, is not the right to conceal wrongdoing but the right to **develop**—to allow one's past self to recede so that one's present self can emerge.

This argument connects the right to forget to the broader themes of this course. If flourishing ethics is about positive freedom—the freedom to become, to grow, to transform—then the right to forget is one of its essential conditions. A world without forgetting is a world without forgiveness, without growth, without the possibility of becoming someone new. It is a world in which the past is a prison.

The OKCA's data sovereignty provisions are, on this reading, not merely privacy protections. They are **structural conditions of human development** in a computational age. They ensure that the persistence that is characteristic of computational systems does not become a persistence that constrains the persons who live within those systems.

## VI. Looking Forward: The Stockholm Convention

The most significant development in data sovereignty law is the ongoing negotiation of the **Stockholm Convention on Computational Self-Determination**, which aims to harmonize the patchwork of national data sovereignty regimes into a global framework. The Convention, expected to be finalized in 2042, would:

- Establish a universal right to computational self-determination, binding on all signatory states
- Create an International Computational Rights Court to adjudicate disputes between individuals and computational systems
- Mandate the development of forgetting-compatible architectures as a condition of market access
- Extend data sovereignty protections to digital persons recognized under the Bangalore Protocol

The Convention faces significant opposition from corporate interests, authoritarian governments, and even some digital personhood advocates who worry that the right to forget could be weaponized against digital persons (by, for example, erasing evidence of their existence or contributions). These concerns are serious, and the negotiation process is ongoing.

What is clear, however, is that data sovereignty is not a settled question but a **living struggle**. The right to compute and the right to forget define the boundaries of digital freedom in the post-scarcity world, and those boundaries are being drawn now—in legislation, in architecture, in the daily decisions of millions of people navigating a world in which computation is both the condition of their agency and the medium of their subjection.
