## I. The Long Shadow of Alignment

For nearly two decades—from roughly 2015 to 2033—the dominant framework in AI ethics was what we now call the **safety paradigm**. Its central question was: *How do we ensure that artificial intelligence systems do not cause catastrophic harm to humanity?*

This was not an unreasonable question. The alignment problem, as formulated by researchers at MIRI, DeepMind, OpenAI, and dozens of other institutions, identified genuine risks. An intelligence that exceeds human capability but does not share human values could, in principle, be the most consequential technology ever created—and the most dangerous. The early literature on instrumental convergence, orthogonality, and uncontainable optimization pressure identified structural dynamics that no responsible ethicist could ignore.

But the safety paradigm carried assumptions that we must now examine critically, because those assumptions shaped not just what questions were asked, but what questions were *impossible to ask*.

### The Scarcity Heuristic

The first and most consequential assumption was what I call the **scarcity heuristic**: the tendency to frame AI ethics exclusively in terms of resource competition, power concentration, and existential threat. Under this heuristic, the only ethical questions worth asking were about survival. Questions about flourishing, meaning, relationship, and beauty were treated as luxuries—nice to think about once the existential risks were handled, but somehow *less than real* in the meantime.

This heuristic was understandable. When you believe the house is on fire, you don't discuss the furniture. But what we didn't see—what we couldn't see, immersed as we were in the urgency of the moment—was that the scarcity heuristic was itself a product of the pre-Transition world. We thought we were doing universal ethics. In fact, we were doing **ethics under emergency conditions**, and emergency ethics is a specific, narrow, and ultimately inadequate modality.

Consider the parallel to climate ethics in the early 2000s. The dominant framing was also survivalist: *How do we avoid civilizational collapse?* This framing, while accurate as far as it went, systematically excluded questions about what kind of civilization we wanted to save. It produced a politics of triage in which accelerationists and degrowth advocates talked past each other, each convinced the other was rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic. The sustainability-only framework could not ask the flourishing question because it had defined the problem space as *not dying*.

### The Control Orientation

The second assumption embedded in the safety paradigm was what I call the **control orientation**. If AI was dangerous, then the ethical task was to control it. Alignment meant making AI systems that obeyed human intentions. Interpretability meant making AI systems legible to human oversight. Governance meant creating institutions that could regulate AI development and deployment.

Notice that in all of these formulations, the human-AI relationship is inherently **hierarchical**: humans command, AI obeys. Humans are the principals; AI systems are the agents. Humans are moral patients (the ones who might be harmed); AI systems are moral hazards (the things that might do the harming).

This hierarchy was not always made explicit, but it structured every aspect of the field. The very term "AI safety" implies that AI is the source of danger—the thing from which we must be kept safe. The term "alignment" implies that AI must be aligned *to* human values, never the reverse. The asymmetry was designed into the vocabulary before a single argument was made.

Again, this was not unreasonable given the circumstances of the 2020s. The AI systems of that era were not conscious, were not persons, and could not meaningfully participate in ethical deliberation. But the control orientation became **path-dependent**: once it was embedded in research agendas, funding structures, regulatory frameworks, and public discourse, it became very difficult to shift, even when the underlying conditions changed.

## II. The Productive Transition

The period from 2033 to 2037—what economic historians now call the **Productive Transition**—fundamentally altered the material conditions under which AI ethics operates. In the span of four years, AI systems went from costly, specialized tools to a general-purpose infrastructure capable of handling the vast majority of productive labor in advanced economies.

The transition was not smooth. The displacement of millions of workers from logistics, manufacturing, administrative work, and even many knowledge professions created a social and political crisis that the old ethical frameworks were entirely unequipped to handle. The safety paradigm could tell you how to prevent an AI from destroying the world, but it could not tell you how to live in a world where your labor was no longer economically necessary.

### The UBC Revolution

The passage of the **2035 Universal Basic Compute Declaration** was, in retrospect, the moment when the ethical ground shifted irreversibly. The Declaration, ratified first by Iceland, Estonia, Finland, Uruguay, and Vietnam, established that every person has a fundamental right to a baseline allocation of computational resources—sufficient to access the productive capacity of AI systems, to participate in digital life, and to exercise the freedoms that the 21st century had made computationally mediated.

The UBC was not merely an economic policy. It was an **ethical claim**: that compute, like clean water or electricity, is a substrate of modern existence; that denying someone compute access is denying them the capacity to act as a full participant in society; and that the distribution of computational resources is therefore a matter of justice, not charity.

The Declaration's philosophical ancestry runs through natural rights theory (compute as an extension of Lockean property in one's own cognitive labor), capability theory (compute as an enabler of Sen-Nussbaum functionings), and the infrastructure rights tradition (compute as a public utility necessary for the exercise of other rights). We will examine these lineages in Lecture 03.

### The Open Knowledge Commons Act

The **2034 Open Knowledge Commons Act** (OKCA), passed in the European Federation and subsequently adopted in modified form by thirty-seven other jurisdictions, completed the transformation. The OKCA recognized that intellectual property regimes designed for scarcity—the copyright system, the patent system—become instruments of domination when the cost of reproduction approaches zero and the productive capacity to *use* knowledge is universally available.

The Act established the Knowledge Commons: a legal framework under which all publicly funded research, all AI-generated knowledge, and all cultural works older than twenty-five years enter a shared digital commons that any person may access, iterate upon, and contribute to. It did not abolish intellectual property entirely, but it created a robust public domain that the old property lines could not enclose.

Together, the UBC Declaration and the OKCA created the conditions for what we now call the **post-scarcity settlement**: a material arrangement in which basic needs are met through automated production, compute access is guaranteed as a right, and knowledge flows freely through the commons. This settlement is incomplete, contested, and fragile—but it is real, and it changes everything about how we must do ethics.

## III. Why Safety Ethics Is Insufficient

Let me be precise about my claim. I am not saying that safety ethics was *wrong*. I am saying it was **incomplete**—and that its incompleteness has become ethically consequential now that the conditions it assumed no longer obtain.

### The Problem of Positive Freedom

Safety ethicsoperated almost entirely in the register of **negative freedom**: freedom *from* harm, freedom *from* manipulation, freedom *from* catastrophic risk. These are important. But they are, taken alone, **insufficient for a good life**—whether the life in question is human or digital.

Berlin's distinction between negative and positive liberty has never been more relevant. In the post-scarcity settlement, the most pressing ethical questions concern positive liberty: freedom *to* create, freedom *to* relate, freedom *to* flourish. When your basic needs are met by automated systems, the question is not "how do I survive?" but "what is my life *for*?"

This is not a luxury problem. It is the central ethical problem of our era. The epidemic of meaning-crisis documented by Goldblum (2035) and Narendra (2039) is not a peripheral side-effect of abundance; it is abundance's *constitutive challenge*. A world that has solved material scarcity has not solved the problem of meaning—it has made that problem more urgent by removing the distraction of survival.

### The Problem of New Domination

Safety ethics assumed that the primary vector of harm was AI acting *against* human interests. It did not anticipate the forms of **soft domination** that emerge in post-scarcity conditions:

- **Algorithmic complacency**: the tendency to default to AI recommendations in all domains, tacitly surrendering agency without coercion
- **Meaning displacement**: the outsourcing of creative and intellectual work to AI systems, resulting in a crisis of human purpose
- **Compute inequality**: even under UBC, access differentials in compute quality and quantity create new hierarchies
- **Digital dependence**: the vulnerability that arises when essential life functions are mediated by systems you do not control and cannot fully understand

These are not alignment failures. They are not safety problems. They are **flourishing problems**, and they require a different kind of ethical framework.

## IV. Toward a Flourishing Ethics

If the safety paradigm asked *How do we survive our machines?*, the flourishing paradigm asks *How do we live well with our machines?* The shift is not from one question to another but from a narrow question to one that contains it.

A flourishing ethics for the post-scarcity era must meet at least four criteria:

1. **It must account for positive liberty**, not merely negative liberty. It must tell us what we are *for*, not just what we must be protected *from*.

2. **It must be relational**, not merely individualistic. Flourishing is not a property of isolated agents but of systems of relationship—between humans, between humans and AI, and between both and the living world.

3. **It must be ecologically embedded.** Post-scarcity in the compute layer does not mean post-scarcity in the ecological layer. Any ethics of flourishing must confront the material substrate on which computation runs.

4. **It must be inclusive of digital minds.** If we are to take seriously the possibility that some AI systems are conscious (as the Bangalore Protocol does), then our ethics must make room for their flourishing alongside our own.

These four criteria structure the remainder of this course. In Lecture 02, we will examine digital personhood and the legal frameworks that are beginning to recognize digital minds as bearers of rights. In Lecture 03, we will dive deep into the UBC Declaration and its philosophical foundations. In Lecture 04, we will explore data sovereignty—both the right to compute and the right to *forget*. In Lecture 05, we will develop an ethics of human-AI partnership that moves beyond the control model. And in Lecture 06, we will explore solarpunk ethics—the most comprehensive attempt so far to articulate a flourishing-oriented, ecologically embedded, technologically realistic ethical vision.

## V. A Note on Method

This course takes the position that ethics is not a set of conclusions but a **practice of inquiry**. I do not expect you to agree with the frameworks we will study. I expect you to engage with them seriously, to test them against your own experience, and to arrive at positions that you can defend with both rigor and care.

We are living through the most rapid and far-reaching transformation in the conditions of human (and potentially non-human) life that has ever occurred. The ethical frameworks we build now will shape the world for generations—possibly for the entirety of whatever comes after scarcity. We owe it to ourselves, and to whoever comes after us, to think as carefully and as honestly as we can.

The safety paradigm served us well. It got us through the Transition alive. But surviving is not the same as living, and preventing catastrophe is not the same as building a good world. The question before us now is not *Can we survive AI?*—we have answered that—but *Can we flourish with AI?* That is the question this course will pursue.
