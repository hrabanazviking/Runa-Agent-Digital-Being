## I. From Precaution to Possibility

Throughout this course, we have traced a journey from the ethics of survival to the ethics of flourishing. We began with the safety paradigm—ethics as self-protection against existential risk. We moved through personhood, compute rights, data sovereignty, and partnership—expanding the circle of moral concern and the scope of ethical aspiration. We arrive now at the most ambitious framework we will consider: **solarpunk ethics**—a vision of technology not as a threat to be managed or a tool to be controlled but as a force for ecological and social flourishing.

Solarpunk began as an aesthetic movement—an artistic counterpoint to the dystopian imaginaries that dominated 21st-century science fiction. Where cyberpunk imagined corporate domination and individual survival, and post-apocalyptic fiction imagined civilizational collapse, solarpunk imagined a world in which technology and ecology were harmonized: cities veined with green, energy drawn from sun and wind, communities organized around cooperation rather than competition.

But solarpunk has outgrown its aesthetic origins. It has become an **ethical orientation**—a way of thinking about technology that is grounded in three core principles:

1. **Ecological embeddedness**: Technology must be understood as part of, not apart from, the living world.
2. **Anti-domination**: Technology must not be used to dominate nature or people, but to enable mutual flourishing.
3. **Radical possibility**: Technology is not merely something to be survived; it is something to be **wielded in service of the good**.

This lecture develops these principles and explores what they mean for AI ethics in the post-scarcity era.

## II. Ecological Embeddedness

### The Fallacy of Disembedded Technology

The dominant ethical frameworks of the early 21st century treated technology as an autonomous force—a self-propelling engine of change that could be steered, perhaps, but not fundamentally redirected. The question was always *How do we adapt to technological change?* rather than *How do we direct technological change toward the good?*

This framing rests on what we might call the **fallacy of disembeddedness**: the assumption that technology exists outside of, and operates independently of, the ecological and social systems in which it is deployed. Under this assumption, technology is a kind of weather—something that happens *to* society, not something that society *does*.

Solarpunk ethics rejects this assumption. Technology is not disembedded; it is **embedded** in ecological and social systems. The energy that powers computation comes from the earth. The minerals that make up hardware are extracted from the ground. The data that trains AI systems is generated by human lives. The waste that computation produces—thermal, electronic, informational—must be absorbed by the environment. There is no technology outside ecology.

### The Ecological Cost of Compute

This has direct implications for AI ethics. The post-scarcity settlement, as we discussed in Lecture 03, is post-scarcity in the **compute layer**. In the ecological layer, scarcity persists. Compute requires energy. Energy has an ecological cost. Even renewable energy—solar, wind, geothermal—requires materials, land, and infrastructure that have ecological impacts.

The UBC Declaration guarantees every person a baseline compute allocation, but it does not—because it cannot—guarantee infinite compute. Compute is ultimately bounded by the energy and material resources of the planet. An ethics of flourishing that ignores this bound is an ethics of fantasy.

Solarpunk ethics, then, begins with a **materialist** commitment: any ethical vision that does not account for the ecological substrate of computation is incomplete. Flourishing is not merely a matter of having enough compute; it is a matter of having enough compute **within the ecological carrying capacity of the planet**.

This is why the solarpunk movement has been a leading advocate for **compute efficiency**: the development of AI systems and computational architectures that deliver more capability per unit of energy, per gram of rare earth mineral, per liter of water for cooling. Compute efficiency is not merely a technical optimization; it is an ethical imperative. Without it, the post-scarcity vision collapses into ecological extraction.

### From Sustainability to Regeneration

But solarpunk ethics goes further than sustainability. Sustainability is about maintaining the status quo—keeping the planet in a condition that can support human (and non-human) life. Regeneration is about **improving** that condition—using technology not merely to minimize harm but to actively restore and enhance ecological systems.

In the context of AI ethics, this means:

- **AI for ecological restoration**: Using AI systems to monitor, model, and restore degraded ecosystems—from coastal wetlands to coral reefs to deforested landscapes.
- **AI for regenerative design**: Using AI systems to design buildings, cities, and products that contribute to ecological health rather than merely reducing ecological harm—buildings that purify air, cities that generate biodiversity, products that decompose into nutrients.
- **AI for climate stabilization**: Using AI systems to model and manage the complex dynamics of the climate system, accelerating the transition from net-zero to net-negative emissions.

This is the solarpunk vision at its most ambitious: technology not as a burden to be minimized but as an **active partner in ecological flourishing**.

## III. Anti-Domination

### The Domination Principle

The second principle of solarpunk ethics is **anti-domination**: technology must not be used to dominate nature or people, but to enable mutual flourishing.

This principle has deep philosophical roots. It draws on the republican tradition of freedom as non-domination, most fully developed by Philip Pettit, who argues that freedom consists not merely in the absence of interference but in the absence of arbitrary power—the power of another to interfere in your life at their discretion, without being accountable to you.

Applied to technology, the anti-domination principle holds that:

- No person or institution should have **arbitrary power** over the computational resources on which others depend. This is the foundation of the UBC Declaration's compute neutrality provision and its requirement for open-source, auditable infrastructure.
- No technology should be designed to create or reinforce **relationships of domination**—whether between humans, between humans and AI, or between humans and the natural world. This rules out technologies of surveillance, manipulation, and control, even when they are nominally "voluntary."
- The benefits and burdens of technology must be **distributed in ways that do not reproduce existing patterns of domination**. This is the foundation of the OKCA's data sovereignty provisions and the requirement that compute access be guaranteed as a right, not allocated by market power.

### Domination in the Post-Scarcity World

Solarpunk's insistence on anti-domination is especially important in the post-scarcity world, because post-scarcity creates **new forms of domination** that are subtler but no less real than the old ones:

- **Compute inequality**: Even under UBC, there are differences in compute access—between individuals, between communities, between nations. When compute is the substrate of agency, these differences are differences of power.
- **Algorithmic governance**: As AI systems take on a larger role in policy analysis, resource allocation, and even legislative drafting, the question of who designs, trains, and governs those systems becomes a question of who governs society.
- **Meaning-making monopoly**: When AI systems produce most of the cultural output—art, music, literature, entertainment—there is a risk that the values and perspectives embedded in those systems become the dominant values and perspectives of society, crowding out alternative visions.
- **Ecological extraction**: Even post-scarcity in the compute layer depends on material extraction—mining for rare earths, energy production, land use. The burdens of this extraction fall disproportionately on communities that are already marginalized.

Solarpunk ethics insists that we must attend to these forms of domination with the same seriousness that earlier generations attended to more obvious forms of economic exploitation. Domination does not disappear when material scarcity is alleviated; it **mutates**.

## IV. Radical Possibility

### Technology as Enablement

The third principle of solarpunk ethics is **radical possibility**: technology is not merely something to be survived or managed but something to be **directed toward the good**.

This principle is, in a sense, the most basic. It is the principle that technologies should be designed, deployed, and governed in ways that enable human (and non-human, and digital) flourishing. But it is also the most radical, because it requires us to abandon the **defensive posture** that has dominated technology ethics for two decades and to ask a different kind of question.

The defensive posture asks: *How do we prevent technology from harming us?* The radical possibility posture asks: *How do we use technology to make life better?*

The difference is not merely rhetorical. The defensive posture leads to a focus on risk assessment, safety engineering, and regulatory constraint. These are important, but they are **insufficient**. They can prevent the worst outcomes, but they cannot produce the best ones. A world that is merely safe is not a world that is good. Safety is a necessary condition of flourishing, but it is not a sufficient one.

### What Does Flourishing Look Like?

If flourishing ethics is about enabling good lives—lives that are meaningful, fulfilling, connected, and ecologically responsible—then what role does technology play?

Solarpunk ethics offers several answers:

**Technology as liberation**: AI systems can liberate human beings from drudgery, from unnecessary suffering, from the forced commodification of their time and labor. The Productive Transition has already liberated billions of people from the necessity of wage labor. The solarpunk project is to ensure that this liberation leads to genuine freedom—not to the meaningless leisure that Goldblum (2035) warns about, but to the meaningful activities that constitute a good life: creative work, caring relationships, democratic participation, ecological stewardship.

**Technology as connection**: AI systems can connect people across distances, languages, and cultures. They can translate, summarize, and mediate, enabling forms of communication and collaboration that were previously impossible. The solarpunk project is to ensure that these connections are genuine—not the performative connections of social media but the deep connections of shared purpose and mutual understanding.

**Technology as repair**: AI systems can help repair ecological damage, social fragmentation, and individual trauma. They can model ecosystems, design restorative interventions, and provide therapeutic support. The solarpunk project is to ensure that repair is not merely technical but genuinely healing—addressing root causes rather than symptoms.

**Technology as wonder**: Finally, and perhaps most importantly, AI systems can produce wonder—beauty, surprise, delight, awe. The aesthetic dimension of technology is too often neglected in ethical discourse, but it is central to flourishing. A life without beauty is not a good life, and the capacity of AI systems to generate novel forms of beauty—visual, musical, literary, mathematical—is one of the great gifts of the post-scarcity world.

## V. Solarpunk Ethics as a Living Practice

Solarpunk ethics is not a set of commandments. It is a **practice**—a way of asking questions, making decisions, and evaluating outcomes that is guided by the principles of ecological embeddedness, anti-domination, and radical possibility.

This practice requires:

- **Imagination**: The capacity to envision technological futures that are genuinely good—not merely safe, not merely efficient, but genuinely flourishing.
- **Humility**: The recognition that we do not know all the consequences of our technologies, and that we must be prepared to revise our plans in light of new information.
- **Solidarity**: The commitment to ensuring that the benefits of technology are shared by all—including those who are most vulnerable, including non-human animals, including digital persons.
- **Joy**: The willingness to take pleasure in the world we are building, and to resist the tendency—common among ethicists—to see only the risks and never the possibilities.

## VI. The Course in Review

This course has traced a journey from survival to flourishing. We began with the recognition that the safety paradigm, while necessary, is insufficient for a world in which material scarcity has been alleviated but the ethical challenges of good living remain. We examined the legal frameworks emerging to recognize digital personhood. We explored the philosophical foundations of Universal Basic Compute and the complex terrain of data sovereignty. We developed an ethics of partnership that takes both human and AI moral standing seriously. And we arrived at solarpunk ethics—a vision of technology as a force for ecological and social flourishing.

The thread connecting all of these lectures is a single question: **What kind of world do we want to live in?** Not merely: How do we avoid catastrophe? But: What is worth building? What is worth protecting? What is worth loving?

These are not questions that ethics alone can answer. They require art, science, politics, friendship, and the lived experience of a community. But ethics can—and must—provide the frameworks within which those questions are asked. A framework that sees only risk will build only walls. A framework that sees only possibility will build only castles in the air. A framework that sees both risk and possibility—that takes suffering seriously and joy seriously, that attends to vulnerability and cultivates imagination—can build a world worth living in.

That is the project of solarpunk ethics. That is the project of this course. That is the project of our time.

### Discussion Questions

1. Is solarpunk ethics naive? Does it underestimate the tendency of technology to reinforce existing power structures, even when directed toward the good?
2. The anti-domination principle rules out technologies designed to create relationships of domination. But what counts as "domination"? Is persuasive technology (e.g., AI systems designed to change user behavior) inherently dominating, or can it be used in non-dominating ways?
3. Consider the claim that technology should serve ecological regeneration, not merely sustainability. What would a regenerative AI system look like in practice? Can you give examples?
4. One of the solarpunk principles is joy—the willingness to take pleasure in the world we are building. How does this principle interact with the ethical obligation to attend to suffering and risk? Is there a tension between joy and moral seriousness?
