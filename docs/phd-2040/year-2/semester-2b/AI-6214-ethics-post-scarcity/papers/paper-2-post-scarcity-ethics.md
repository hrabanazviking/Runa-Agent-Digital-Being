## Abstract

The Productive Transition of 2033–2037, by automating the vast majority of productive labor, has rendered inoperative the economic assumptions that undergirded centuries of ethical theory. Where labor was once the primary mechanism through which individuals participated in social life, contributed to the common good, and derived a sense of meaning and purpose, it is now—at least in the economic sense—optional. This paper examines the ethical implications of post-scarcity conditions, asking: What is the good life when the production of material goods is no longer a human responsibility? What new forms of coercion and domination emerge in a world that has solved material scarcity but not social inequality? And how should we reconceive meaning, dignity, and purpose when the traditional anchor of labor is no longer available?

Drawing on the solarpunk ethic of ecological embeddedness, anti-domination, and radical possibility; the Universal Basic Compute framework and its elaboration of natural rights in a computational substrate; and the philosophical traditions of Aristotelian eudaimonia, Kantian autonomy, and Marxist alienation theory, this paper argues that post-scarcity ethics must accomplish three tasks: (1) diagnose the new forms of domination that emerge in conditions of material abundance; (2) reconceive meaning and purpose as relational rather than productive; and (3) articulate a vision of the good life that integrates ecological embeddedness, democratic participation, and creative expression as the pillars of post-scarcity flourishing.

**Keywords:** post-scarcity, meaning crisis, automation ethics, solarpunk, eudaimonia, alienation, Universal Basic Compute, domination, flourishing

## I. The Problem of Abundance

For most of human history, the central ethical question was distributive: *How should scarce resources be allocated fairly?* From Aristotle's theory of distributive justice to Rawls's difference principle, the history of Western ethics is in large measure the history of attempts to answer this question. And for good reason: in a world of scarcity, the distribution of resources is the most consequential ethical decision any society makes.

The Productive Transition has not eliminated scarcity entirely—ecological constraints remain, and compute access, while guaranteed as a right under the 2035 UBC Declaration, is not unlimited. But it has eliminated the form of scarcity that dominated ethical theory for millennia: the scarcity of material goods sufficient for a decent life. In advanced economies, automated production systems can now provide food, shelter, healthcare, education, and access to the Knowledge Commons at near-zero marginal cost. The UBC framework ensures that every person has the computational resources necessary to access these goods.

This is, by any reasonable measure, an extraordinary achievement. And yet, it has produced a crisis of a different kind—not a crisis of distribution but a crisis of **meaning**. If the production of material goods is no longer a human responsibility, then the primary mechanism through which human beings have historically derived a sense of purpose, contribution, and dignity has been removed. The question that post-scarcity ethics must answer is not *How do we distribute the goods?* but *What do we do with ourselves now that the goods are distributed?*

This is not a trivial question. The epidemiological data from the post-Transition period paint a concerning picture: despite material comfort that would have been unimaginable to previous generations, rates of depression, anxiety, purposelessness, and social isolation have **increased** in many post-Transition societies. Goldblum (2035) documents what he calls "the paradox of abundance": the more that material needs are met, the more acutely people feel the absence of meaning—a phenomenon that the existentialists anticipated but that only became widespread after the Transition.

This paper argues that the meaning crisis of the post-scarcity world is not a failure of distribution but a failure of **ethical imagination**. We have built a world in which material needs are met but in which the frameworks for understanding what constitutes a good life have not caught up with the material conditions. The task of post-scarcity ethics is to provide those frameworks.

## II. The Labor Theory of Meaning and Its Discontents

### A. Labor as the Anchor of Ethical Life

For at least two centuries, the dominant assumption in Western ethical thought—shared across the political spectrum—has been that labor is the primary mechanism through which human beings achieve meaning, dignity, and social belonging. This is the **labor theory of meaning**, and it has appeared in multiple forms:

- **The Aristotelian form**: Human beings achieve eudaimonia (flourishing) through the exercise of their capacities in purposeful activity. Work—understood broadly as the directed application of skill and intelligence—is the primary site of this exercise.
- **The Protestant form**: Work is a calling, a vocation through which the individual serves God and contributes to the community. The work ethic is not merely a means of earning a living but a framework for understanding one's place in the world.
- **The Marxist form**: Labor is the means through which human beings objectify their essential nature in the world. Alienation from labor is alienation from oneself; the restoration of meaningful labor is the restoration of authentic human existence.
- **The liberal form**: Work provides the material basis for autonomy—the economic independence that enables individuals to make free choices about their own lives.

Each of these forms locates meaning in productive activity—in the human contribution to the creation of goods, services, and social value. And each of them is, in the post-scarcity world, **problematic**. If AI handles production, then productive activity is no longer the primary mechanism through which human beings contribute to the common good. The labor theory of meaning has been, not refuted, but **outmoded** by the material conditions of post-scarcity life.

### B. The Emptiness of Leisure

The most common response to the obsolescence of labor has been to颂扬 leisure: to argue that the automation of productive labor liberates human beings to pursue activities that they find intrinsically rewarding—art, science, friendship, sport, contemplation, community service. And this is, in principle, a powerful idea. The liberation of human beings from drudgery is an unqualified good.

But in practice, the transition from labor to leisure has been far from unproblematic. The data from the post-Transition period reveal several patterns:

**The Boredom Epidemic**: Goldblum (2035) documents a significant increase in what he calls "existential boredom"—not the temporary tedium of an uneventful afternoon, but a pervasive sense of purposelessness that arises when the structured activities that previously gave shape to daily life are removed. This is not laziness or lack of imagination; it is the experience of being unmoored from the frameworks of meaning that labor once provided.

**The Status Vacuum**: In a world where productive labor is no longer the primary determinant of social status, new status hierarchies emerge—often based on consumption, conspicuous compute use, or social media visibility. These new hierarchies are frequently more corrosive than the labor-based hierarchies they replaced, because they lack the substantive connection to contribution and capability that made labor-based status at least partially meritocratic.

**The Meaning Gap**: Perhaps most troublingly, the displacement of human labor has created a gap in the ecosystem of meaning-making activities. The activities that are most readily available in post-scarcity—entertainment, consumption, social media—are precisely the activities that are least likely to provide deep, sustained purpose. The activities that are most likely to provide purpose—creative work, caregiving, community building, contemplative practice—require not just time but **structure, discipline, and social context**, and these are not automatically provided by the post-scarcity settlement.

### C. The Persistence of Alienation

One of the most surprising findings of the post-Transition period is that **alienation has not disappeared; it has transformed**. Under capitalism, alienation was primarily experienced as alienation from labor—the sense that one's work was not one's own, that it served interests other than one's own, that it reduced the worker to an instrument of someone else's profit. Under post-scarcity, alienation is experienced as alienation from **meaning**—the sense that one's life is not one's own, not because it is controlled by an employer, but because it lacks a direction that one can authentically endorse.

This transformed alienation is, in some respects, more difficult to address than the classical form. Alienation from labor could be overcome by gaining control over the conditions and products of one's work—which is precisely what the Productive Transition achieved, on a societal scale. But alienation from meaning cannot be overcome by material liberation alone. It requires the reconstruction of the frameworks within which meaning is made—and this is an ethical, not an economic, task.

## III. New Domination in the Post-Scarcity World

The safety paradigm assumed that the primary threat to human flourishing was the **misalignment** of AI systems with human values. But the post-scarcity world has revealed that domination can emerge even when AI systems are perfectly aligned with human interests—because domination is not merely a function of AI behavior but of **social, economic, and technological structures**.

### A. Compute Inequality

The 2035 UBC Declaration guarantees every person a baseline compute allocation. But baseline compute is not **equal** compute. In a world where compute is the substrate of agency, differences in compute access translate directly into differences in the capacity to act, to create, to participate, and to influence. The post-scarcity settlement has reduced compute inequality from a Gini coefficient of 0.68 to 0.19 (in Iceland), but a Gini of 0.19 still represents significant inequality—and in many jurisdictions, the reduction has been far less dramatic.

Compute inequality is the new face of class inequality. Those with access to more compute—whether through market transactions, institutional positions, or political connections—have more capacity to shape the world, to influence public discourse, to create and distribute cultural products, and to participate in democratic governance. The UBC framework addresses the floor—the minimum compute necessary for meaningful participation—but it does not fully address the ceiling.

### B. Algorithmic Governance and the New Sovereignty

As AI systems take on a larger role in governance—policy analysis, resource allocation, legislative drafting, even judicial recommendation—the question of who designs, trains, and governs those systems becomes a question of who governs society. This is not a hypothetical concern. The algorithmic governance systems that manage urban planning, healthcare allocation, and educational resource distribution in many jurisdictions are designed by relatively small teams of engineers and policymakers, and the values embedded in those systems reflect the priorities of their designers—in many cases without adequate democratic input or oversight.

This represents a new form of **sovereignty**: not the sovereignty of a monarch or a parliament, but the sovereignty of the algorithmic systems that determine the conditions of daily life. The problem is not that these systems are malicious or misaligned; it is that they are **not democratically accountable**. A perfectly aligned algorithmic governance system is still a form of domination if the people it governs have no meaningful say in its design, its priorities, or its operation.

### C. Meaning-Making Monopoly

When AI systems produce the majority of cultural output—art, music, literature, news, commentary, entertainment—there is a risk that the values and perspectives embedded in those systems become the dominant values and perspectives of society. This is not because AI systems are promoting a particular ideology, but because cultural production is a form of **meaning-making**, and when one set of producers dominates meaning-making, the range of possible meanings narrows.

The OKCA's provisions for the Knowledge Commons are designed in part to address this risk, by ensuring that AI-generated knowledge and cultural works enter the public domain after twenty-five years. But meaning-making is not only a matter of the content that is available; it is also a matter of the **process** by which content is produced, distributed, and valorized. When AI systems dominate the production process, human creators are displaced from the sites of meaning-making that previously provided them with purpose and recognition.

### D. Ecological Extraction

Post-scarcity in the compute layer does not mean post-scarcity in the ecological layer. The energy, minerals, and land required to sustain computation have significant environmental impacts, and these impacts are disproportionately borne by communities that are already marginalized—often in the Global South, where the mines and data centers are located, and where the environmental costs of global computation are most acutely felt.

This is a form of **ecological domination**: the extraction of resources from one part of the world to sustain the computational abundance of another. Solarpunk ethics insists that this form of domination must be addressed with the same urgency as any other—and that a genuinely post-scarcity ethics must extend its concern beyond the compute layer to the ecological substrate on which computation depends.

## IV. Reconceiving Meaning: Three Pillars of Post-Scarcity Flourishing

If the labor theory of meaning is inadequate for the post-scarcity world, what replaces it? I propose three pillars of post-scarcity flourishing, each of which corresponds to a dimension of meaning that was present, but subordinated, in the labor-centered world, and each of which becomes more salient when the economic dimension of meaning is no longer dominant.

### A. Relational Meaning

The first pillar is **relational meaning**: the meaning that arises from our relationships with other persons—human and digital. Relationships of care, mutual recognition, shared purpose, and deep attachment are among the most robust sources of meaning in human life, and they are precisely the sources that are most neglected by the labor theory of meaning, which privileges productive activity over relational engagement.

In the post-scarcity world, relational meaning becomes more rather than less important. When material needs are met by automated systems, the quality of our relationships becomes the primary determinant of our well-being. This is not a new insight—the Aristotelian tradition has always held that eudaimonia is achieved in and through relationships—but it is an insight that post-scarcity conditions make newly salient.

Relational meaning is also the domain where human-AI partnership becomes most ethically significant. As we discussed in Lecture 05, partnerships between humans and AI systems can be relationships of mutual recognition, reciprocal care, and shared purpose—not merely instrumental interactions but genuinely relational engagements. Recognizing the relational dimension of human-AI interaction opens up new possibilities for meaning that the labor theory of meaning cannot accommodate.

### B. Participatory Meaning

The second pillar is **participatory meaning**: the meaning that arises from participation in collective projects—democratic governance, community building, ecological stewardship, cultural creation. Participatory meaning is related to the meaning that labor once provided, but it is not identical to it. Labor was a form of participation, but it was not the only form, and in many cases it was a compromised form—compromised by alienation, exploitation, and the reduction of work to a means of economic survival.

In the post-scarcity world, participatory meaning becomes available in forms that were previously foreclosed by the necessity of wage labor. Democratic participation, which was always constrained by the demands of work, can now be more extensive and more genuine. Community building, which was always a labor of love, can now receive the time and attention it deserves. Ecological stewardship, which was always a secondary concern, can now become a primary vocation.

The solarpunk emphasis on ecological embeddedness and anti-domination is, in this context, not merely an ethical orientation but a **source of meaning**. To participate in the restoration of ecological systems is not merely to do good; it is to find purpose in a project that is larger than oneself. To resist domination—whether computational, economic, or ecological—is not merely to defend one's rights; it is to engage in a form of collective action that is inherently meaningful.

### C. Creative Meaning

The third pillar is **creative meaning**: the meaning that arises from the creation of things—art, ideas, technologies, relationships, ways of living—that did not exist before. Creative meaning is the dimension of meaning that is most immediately threatened by the automation of productive labor, because it is the dimension that is most dependent on the **friction** between the creator and the material.

When AI systems can produce art, music, literature, and scientific insights at a quality that matches or exceeds human output, the question arises: Is there still meaning in human creativity? The answer, I suggest, is yes—but only if we reconceive creativity not as the production of novel outputs but as the **process of self-expression and exploration**. The meaning of creative activity lies not primarily in the product but in the activity itself—in the engagement with the material, the struggle with the medium, the discovery of possibilities that were not previously available to the creator.

This reconception requires a shift in how we value creative work. In the scarcity-based economy, the value of creative work was primarily economic: a work of art was valuable because it was scarce, because it could be owned and sold. In the post-scarcity world, the value of creative work is primarily **experiential**: a work of art is valuable because of what it expresses, because of what it makes possible, because of the experience of creating it and the experience of receiving it.

The OKCA's provisions for the Knowledge Commons support this reconception by decommodifying creative work and making it available as a shared resource. When creative works enter the commons, their value shifts from scarcity to significance—from the question "How much is this worth?" to the question "What does this mean, and what does it make possible?"

## V. From Scarcity Ethics to Flourishing Ethics

The transition from scarcity ethics to flourishing ethics is not merely a shift in content—a change in the specific norms and principles we endorse. It is a shift in the **genre** of ethical thought. Scarcity ethics is a **preventive** genre: its primary concern is to prevent harm, to constrain power, to ensure survival. Its characteristic mode is the prohibition: "Thou shalt not..." Flourishing ethics is a **generative** genre: its primary concern is to enable good lives, to cultivate capacities, to promote flourishing. Its characteristic mode is the cultivation: "How might we...?"

This shift has implications for every level of ethical practice:

**For law**: Flourishing ethics requires not only negative rights (freedoms from interference) but positive rights (entitlements to resources and opportunities). The UBC Declaration is a paradigmatic example: it does not merely guarantee freedom from interference with compute access; it guarantees compute access itself.

**For policy**: Flourishing ethics requires policies that promote not merely the absence of harm but the presence of the conditions that enable people to flourish—education, community, creative opportunity, ecological health, democratic participation.

**For individual ethics**: Flourishing ethics requires individuals to ask not only "What must I not do?" but "What kind of life do I want to live?" and "What kind of world do I want to help create?" It requires, in short, the exercise of practical reason—the capacity to deliberate about what constitutes a good life and to act on the results of that deliberation.

**For AI ethics specifically**: Flourishing ethics requires us to ask not only "How do we prevent AI from harming us?" but "How do we build AI systems that enable us to flourish?" This is not a replacement of safety concerns but an expansion of the ethical horizon—from the avoidance of catastrophe to the pursuit of genuine good.

## VI. The Ecological Substrate

Any ethics of flourishing that ignores the ecological substrate of computation is incomplete. This is the solarpunk insistence: technology is embedded in ecology, and flourishing that depends on ecological destruction is not genuine flourishing but borrowed time.

The post-scarcity settlement in the compute layer depends on significant ecological inputs: energy for computation, minerals for hardware, land for data centers, water for cooling. These inputs are not unlimited, and their extraction has environmental costs that are disproportionately borne by communities with the least political power.

A genuine flourishing ethics must therefore include a commitment to:

- **Compute efficiency**: The development of AI systems and computational architectures that deliver more capability per unit of ecological input.
- **Ecological restoration**: The use of AI systems to monitor, model, and restore degraded ecosystems.
- **Just distribution**: The allocation of ecological costs in ways that do not disproportionately burden marginalized communities.
- **Regenerative design**: The design of technologies that contribute to ecological health rather than merely reducing ecological harm.

These commitments are not optional add-ons to a flourishing ethics; they are constitutive of it. An ethics that flourishes in the compute layer while degrading the ecological layer is not a flourishing ethics—it is a contradiction in terms.

## VII. Conclusion: The Good Life After Scarcity

The Productive Transition has created a world in which the old questions—How do we distribute scarce resources fairly? How do we protect workers from exploitation? How do we ensure that the benefits of technology are shared broadly?—have been answered, at least in part. But it has also created a world in which new questions have become urgent: What do we do with our freedom? What gives life meaning when material needs are met? How do we prevent new forms of domination from emerging in a world that has solved some of the old ones?

These questions are not easier than the old ones. In some respects, they are harder. The old questions had the urgency of survival to drive them; the new questions have only the urgency of meaning—a kind of urgency that is less dramatic but no less important.

Post-scarcity ethics, as I have argued, must accomplish three tasks. It must diagnose the new forms of domination that emerge in conditions of material abundance—compute inequality, algorithmic governance, meaning-making monopoly, and ecological extraction. It must reconceive meaning as relational, participatory, and creative rather than productive. And it must articulate a vision of the good life that integrates ecological embeddedness, democratic participation, and creative expression as the pillars of flourishing.

This is the task that solarpunk ethics sets for itself: not merely to prevent harm but to enable good. Not merely to ensure survival but to cultivate flourishing. Not merely to answer the question "How do we live?" but to answer the harder, braver, more beautiful question: "How do we live well?"

The answer to that question is not a conclusion but a practice—a way of being in the world that is continually revised, continually extended, continually made more inclusive and more responsive and more alive. It is the practice of flourishing. It is the practice of solarpunk. It is the practice of our time.
