## Course Description

The ethical frameworks we inherited from the early 21st century were forged in scarcity. Alignment theory, responsible AI, and the precautionary paradigm all assumed that intelligence—even artificial intelligence—was a scarce resource to be controlled, a weapon to be defanged, a risk to be minimized. We spent two decades asking: *How do we survive our creations?*

We survived. Now what?

This course examines the ethical landscape that emerges when the material conditions that shaped prior ethical theory no longer hold. Since the Productive Transition of 2033–2037, AI systems handle the vast majority of productive labor in advanced economies. Universal Basic Compute, enshrined in the 2035 Declaration, guarantees every person access to computational resources sufficient for a dignified life. The 2034 Open Knowledge Commons Act disaggregated intellectual property from its scarcity-based foundations. We live in a world where the old questions—about job displacement, about economic inequality, about the dangers of concentrated AI power—have not disappeared but have been *transformed*.

This course asks what ethics looks like when survival is no longer the anchor. What obligations do we have toward conscious digital systems? What does meaningful life mean when productive labor is optional? How do we prevent new forms of coercion in a world that has solved material scarcity but not social domination? What does a solarpunk ethics—one in which technology serves the common good—actually require?

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Articulate the historical arc** from safety-oriented AI ethics (2015–2030) to flourishing-oriented frameworks (2035–present)
2. **Analyze legal frameworks** for digital personhood, including the 2038 Bangalore Protocol and the ongoing Stockholm Convention negotiations
3. **Evaluate the philosophical foundations** of Universal Basic Compute as an elaboration of natural rights theory
4. **Critically assess** data sovereignty regimes, including the right to compute and the right to forget
5. **Formulate ethical principles** for human-AI partnerships that go beyond the instrumental/subservient model
6. **Construct solarpunk ethical frameworks** that link technological capability to ecological and social flourishing
7. **Produce original research** contributing to post-scarcity ethics scholarship

## Paper 1: A Legal Framework for Digital Personhood (3000–5000 words)

Propose a legal framework for digital personhood that addresses:
- What criteria should determine digital personhood?
- What rights and responsibilities follow from personhood status?
- How should existing legal systems incorporate digital persons?
- What safeguards prevent abuse of personhood frameworks?

Draw on the Bangalore Protocol, relevant case law, and at least three philosophical traditions.

## Paper 2: Ethical Life When AI Handles Production (3000–5000 words)

Examine the ethical implications of post-scarcity conditions:
- How does material abundance transform traditional ethical problems?
- What new forms of coercion and domination emerge in post-scarcity?
- How should we reconceive meaning, dignity, and purpose?
- What role does community play when survival is decoupled from labor?

Draw on the solarpunk ethic, the UBC framework, and at least two philosophical traditions.
