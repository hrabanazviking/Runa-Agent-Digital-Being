## Course Description

The era of million-agent deployments is no longer theoretical. From the 2034 Sahara Reforestation Project's coordination of 12 million autonomous agents to global supply-chain orchestrations running 50+ million agents across jurisdictions, planetary-scale multi-agent systems pose challenges that break every assumption of classical distributed systems: heterogeneous trust, adversarial economics, partial observability at continental scale, and the need for consensus among agents that may not share a protocol suite.

This course provides a rigorous treatment of the architectures, protocols, and mathematical frameworks needed to orchestrate AI agents at planetary scale. We draw on distributed consensus theory, mechanism design, information economics, and real-world case studies—most extensively, the Sahara Reforestation Project (2034–2039), the largest multi-agent deployment in history.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Analyze** hierarchical, flat, and hybrid orchestration patterns and select appropriate architectures for given deployment constraints.
2. **Design** agent economies with stable equilibria, including reputation systems, credit networks, and trust propagation.
3. **Evaluate** consensus protocols for global-scale deployments under adversarial and churn-heavy conditions.
4. **Formulate** resource allocation problems at planetary scale using stochastic optimization and market-based mechanisms.
5. **Implement** the Common Agent Protocol (CAP/2034) and reason about its correctness properties.
6. **Synthesize** lessons from the Sahara Reforestation Project into design principles for future planetary-scale systems.

## Research Papers

### Paper 1: Design Principles for Stable Agent Economies
A 3,000–5,000 word research paper proposing and defending a set of design principles for agent economies that remain stable under adversarial pressure, scale stress, and information asymmetry. Must engage with at least two reputation/trust frameworks and include formal or simulation-based analysis.

### Paper 2: Technical Analysis of the Sahara Reforestation Project
A 3,000–5,000 word research paper analyzing a specific technical component of the Sahara Reforestation Project (e.g., consensus protocol choice, resource allocation mechanism, economic incentives for local-agent cooperation). Must include original analysis—not merely a summary of published reports.
