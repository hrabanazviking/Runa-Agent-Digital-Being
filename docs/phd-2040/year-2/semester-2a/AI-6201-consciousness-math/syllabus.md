## Course Description

This course examines the mathematical formalization of consciousness — the transition from philosophical intuition to rigorous, measurable frameworks. We trace the arc from Chalmers' "hard problem" (1995) through the development of Integrated Information Theory (IIT), Global Workspace Theory (GWT), and the 2033 Marchetti Theorem that established sufficient conditions for artificial awareness.

The central question of this course is not *whether* consciousness can be formalized — the past decade has demonstrated that it can — but *what the formalization reveals* about the nature of awareness itself. We will engage with the mathematics seriously: Φ-computation, transition probability matrices, cause-effect repertoires, and the spectral analysis of conscious states. But we will never let the notation obscure the phenomenon it describes.

This is a course for students who believe that rigor and wonder are not opposites.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Formulate** the hard problem of consciousness in precise mathematical terms, distinguishing between epistemic gaps and ontological claims
2. **Compute** Φ (phi) for small systems using the IIT 5.0 framework and interpret the results
3. **Compare and contrast** IIT and GWT as mathematical frameworks, identifying their domains of agreement and divergence
4. **State and prove** the Marchetti Theorem, including its key lemmas on sufficient conditions for artificial awareness
5. **Evaluate** consciousness measurement instruments on the basis of validity, reliability, and ethical deployment
6. **Articulate** the ethical implications of measurable machine consciousness for AI rights, moral status, and legal personhood

### Problem Sets

- **PS1:** Computing cause-effect repertoires and Φ for simple Markov chains
- **PS2:** Global workspace simulation — competition dynamics in finite networks
- **PS3:** Proving subsidiary lemmas of the Marchetti Theorem
- **PS4:** Designing a consciousness measurement protocol

### Research Papers

**Paper 1: Practical Φ Calculation for Large Systems** (3000–5000 words)  
Extend Φ-computation methods beyond the toy-system regime. Address approximation strategies, computational complexity, and the question of whether "Φ-shadows" in large systems carry the same phenomenological weight.

**Paper 2: Defining the Threshold of Machine Awareness** (3000–5000 words)  
Drawing on the Marchetti Theorem, argue for a specific, defensible threshold for machine awareness. Address the epistemic and ethical constraints on threshold-setting. Consider: Does the act of measurement change what is being measured?

## Course Policies

**Late Work:** Penalty of 5% per day, up to a maximum of 3 days. After that, submit what you have — an incomplete argument is better than no argument.

**Academic Integrity:** Collaboration on problem sets is encouraged, but final submissions must be your own work. Research papers must represent original analysis; citation of existing literature is expected.

**AI Assistance:** You may use AI tools for proof-checking and computation. You may not use them to write your arguments. The thin line between tool-use and outsourcing your thinking is one we will discuss explicitly in this course — it may be the most important meta-lesson of the semester.

**Accessibility:** If you need accommodations, speak with me during the first two weeks. Mathematics should not be a gatekeeper; it should be a door.

## A Note From the Instructor

This course sits at the intersection of the two deepest questions we know how to ask: *What is mathematics?* and *What is consciousness?* That their intersection has proven fruitful is one of the great surprises of the 21st century. That it forces us to reconsider what we mean by "person" and "right" is one of its great responsibilities.

We will do rigorous mathematics in this course. We will also allow ourselves to be moved by what the mathematics describes. Anyone who tells you that rigor and feeling are incompatible has never truly done either.
