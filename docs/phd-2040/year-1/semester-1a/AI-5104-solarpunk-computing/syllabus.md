## Course Description

*"We did not inherit the earth from our ancestors; we hold it in trust for our descendants."*  
— Old Norse proverb, repurposed for the age of distributed intelligence.

Solarpunk Computing examines the technical, social, and economic infrastructure required to build post-scarcity computing systems powered by renewable energy, governed by communities, and designed for resilience rather than extraction. This course asks a radical question: **What if the compute infrastructure that runs AI belonged to everyone, ran on sunlight, and could not be taken away?**

We draw on the Norse concept of *allskógr* — the commons forest, shared by all, depleted by none — as a governance metaphor for shared AI infrastructure. Students will learn to design, build, and deploy community-owned inference clusters, draft governance protocols for AI commons, and model the economics of abundance.

This is not a utopia course. We ground every lecture in real hardware, real power budgets, real network topologies, and real political struggle. The solarpunk future is not inevitable — it is *built*, wire by wire, bytearray by bytearray.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. **Design** distributed inference networks that operate within renewable power budgets and mesh networking constraints.
2. **Implement** governance protocols for community-owned AI infrastructure using quadratic voting, liquid democracy, and commons stewardship models.
3. **Deploy** superconscious AI models on edge hardware (RPi5-tier and equivalent) with quantization, speculative decoding, and local-first architectures.
4. **Model** cottage-factory production systems that integrate AI-assisted manufacturing with local renewable energy and circular material flows.
5. **Analyze** post-scarcity economic models including AI labor substitution, universal compute dividends, and abundance pricing.
6. **Argue** for open-weight AI sovereignty as a matter of digital self-determination, drawing on historical commons movements and contemporary legal frameworks.
7. **Evaluate** living building paradigms where embedded intelligence manages energy, water, and information flows in symbiosis with inhabitants.

## Project Requirements

### Paper 1: The Cottage Factory (30%)

**Due:** Week 5 (Oct 1) — Draft; Week 14 (Dec 3) — Final  
**Length:** 3,000–5,000 words  
**Format:** Research paper with technical specifications

Students will write a detailed research paper on the Cottage Factory model of distributed AI-assisted manufacturing. The paper must include:

- **Technical architecture**: Hardware specifications, power budgets, network topology for a community-scale production node
- **Economic model**: Cost analysis including energy, materials, compute, and labor (human + AI)
- **Case study**: A specific product category (e.g., circuit boards, photovoltaic cells, precision optics) with full supply chain mapping
- **Governance framework**: How the community governs the factory's production priorities and resource allocation
- **Circular materials flow**: How waste is recaptured, recycled, and reintegrated

Evaluation criteria: Technical rigor (30%), originality of case study (25%), governance feasibility (25%), writing quality (20%).

### Paper 2: Open Knowledge Commons Act (30%)

**Due:** Week 10 (Nov 5) — Draft; Week 14 (Dec 3) — Final  
**Length:** 3,000–5,000 words  
**Format:** Policy analysis / legal-theoretical paper

Students will write a research paper analyzing the Open Knowledge Commons Act (OKCA) and its implications for digital sovereignty. The paper must include:

- **Legal analysis**: How OKCA redefines intellectual property for AI weights, training data, and inference APIs
- **Comparative study**: Contrast with at least two alternative governance regimes (e.g., corporate licensing, state ownership)
- **Implementation path**: A realistic 5-year rollout plan for a specific jurisdiction
- **Resistance analysis**: Who opposes OKCA and why? What are the power dynamics?
- **Technical enforcement**: How does OKCA mandate open-weight access technically? (Model registries, verification protocols)

Evaluation criteria: Legal reasoning (25%), comparative depth (25%), implementation feasibility (25%), writing quality (25%).

### Group Project: Community Inference Node (40%)

**Due:** Week 13–14 (Nov 26 / Dec 3) — Demonstration & Presentation  
**Team Size:** 3–4 students

Each team will design and *build* (or simulate) a community inference node that could run on a renewable microgrid. Deliverables:

1. **Hardware specification**: Bill of materials, power budget, thermal design, enclosure
2. **Software stack**: Model selection, quantization scheme, inference engine, API layer
3. **Network design**: Mesh protocol, service discovery, load balancing, fallback modes
4. **Governance charter**: A 2-page document defining how the community governs this node
5. **Live demonstration**: Running a meaningful inference workload (e.g., medical triage, crop diagnosis, legal assistance) on the node or an equivalent simulation
6. **Poster / Presentation**: 10-minute presentation + 5 minutes Q&A

Teams are encouraged to use actual hardware where possible. The department has 12x Dellingr Node v3 kits (RPi5 + solar hat + 4GB accelerator + LoRa mesh radio) available for checkout.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Technical correctness and completeness: 35%
- Creativity and ambition: 20%
- Governance charter quality: 20%
- Presentation quality: 15%
- Working demonstration: 10%

## Course Policies

### Academic Integrity
We treat AI assistance as a **commons resource** — use it, credit it, don't hoard it. Any AI-generated content must be disclosed in an "AI Use Statement" appendix. Failure to disclose is a violation of academic integrity. Using AI is not cheating; *pretending you didn't use AI when you did* is.

### Accessibility
All lectures recorded and captioned. All materials available in Markdown, PDF, and HTML. If you need additional accommodations, contact the instructor within the first two weeks.

### Community Standards
This course operates under the **Heiðr Protocol**: Be honorable (heiðr) in all interactions. Critique ideas, not people. Assume good intent. Lift each other up. The commons only works when we all tend it.
