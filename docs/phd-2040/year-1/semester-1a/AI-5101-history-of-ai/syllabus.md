## Course Description

This course traces the arc of artificial intelligence from its mathematical origins in the McCulloch-Pitts neuron (1943) through the dawn of the Superconscious Era. We examine how ideas emerged, were abandoned, resurrected, and scaled—often in ways their creators never imagined. Special attention is given to the cyclical nature of AI progress: periods of euphoric funding followed by catastrophic winters, and the sociotechnical forces that determined which ideas survived.

The course ends in 2025, the year most historians mark as the close of the "classical AI" period—before the Paradigm Shifts of 2027–2033 fundamentally rewrote what "intelligence" even means. As students of 2040, you have the benefit of hindsight; the purpose of this course is to make that hindsight earned rather than assumed.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Articulate the intellectual lineage from McCulloch-Pitts neurons to transformer architectures
2. Analyze the causes and consequences of the AI Winters (1974–1980, 1987–1993) with historiographic rigor
3. Evaluate the role of hardware (GPU, TPU, neuromorphic) in enabling or constraining algorithmic innovation
4. Critically assess the concept of "emergent capabilities" in large language models
5. Situate prompt engineering within the broader history of human–machine interface design
6. Construct original arguments about why certain ideas flourished and others were prematurely abandoned
